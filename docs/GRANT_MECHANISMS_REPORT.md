## Top 5 Mechanisms: Detailed Proposal Strategies

### 1. NSF HBCU-UP Targeted Infusion Project (TIP)

**Why this is the top choice:** This mechanism was designed precisely for projects like ChemSim. It funds short-term, well-defined goals that enhance undergraduate STEM education at HBCUs. Cheyney University is an eligible HBCU, and the project directly modernizes chemistry curriculum through interactive technology. The Alcorn State / Lobaki VR chemistry project (see Section 3) was funded through this exact mechanism.

**What the proposal should emphasize:**
- ChemSim as a tool to modernize chemistry instruction at Cheyney, a resource-constrained HBCU where students may lack access to advanced laboratory equipment
- Browser-based delivery as a low-barrier approach: no specialized hardware required, accessible on student laptops and phones
- The open-source, Creative Commons-licensed model, enabling other HBCUs to adopt the tool at zero cost
- Student retention and success metrics in gateway chemistry courses
- Alignment with HBCU-UP's mission to increase the quality of STEM education and the number of students who pursue STEM degrees at HBCUs

**Budget strategy:** Request close to the $400,000 maximum over 3 years. Allocate funds for: PI summer salary, a student research assistant team (4-6 undergraduates per year), software development support, user testing, assessment instruments, and dissemination.

**Timeline:** Submit Letter of Intent by Fourth Tuesday in July 2026. Full proposal due Second Thursday in November 2026.

---

### 2. NSF IUSE: EDU (Engaged Student Learning, Level 1)

**Why this ranks highly:** IUSE is the broadest NSF mechanism for improving undergraduate STEM education. It funds development, testing, and deployment of curricular innovations that improve learning, persistence, and retention. ChemSim fits squarely within the "Engaged Student Learning" track. Unlike HBCU-UP, IUSE is open to all institutions, so Cheyney could also partner with a research university for complementary expertise.

**What the proposal should emphasize:**
- Evidence-based design: ChemSim's interactive simulations grounded in research on active learning, spatial reasoning, and constructivist pedagogy
- Assessment plan: pre/post testing of conceptual understanding (e.g., using validated instruments like the Chemistry Concepts Inventory), comparison with traditional instruction
- Transferability: browser-based architecture means any institution can deploy ChemSim; open-source code enables community contributions
- The project's potential to serve as a model for simulation-based learning in other STEM disciplines
- Broader impacts through serving underrepresented students at an HBCU

**Budget strategy:** Request up to $400,000 over 3 years. Include costs for educational research assessment, an external evaluator, and conference presentations.

**Timeline:** ESL Level 1 proposals due Third Wednesday in January annually. Target January 2027 submission.

---

### 3. NSF RITEL (Research on Innovative Technologies for Enhanced Learning)

**Why this ranks highly:** RITEL explicitly funds "immersive or augmenting technologies" for education. ChemSim's interactive 3D molecular visualization and planned WebXR/VR capabilities directly align. RITEL supports early-stage research, making it ideal for a project that is still in active development. Awards go up to $900,000, providing substantial resources.

**What the proposal should emphasize:**
- The research question: How do interactive, browser-based molecular simulations affect conceptual understanding of intermolecular forces and chemical bonding compared to static representations?
- Technology innovation: WebXR integration enabling seamless transition from 2D browser to immersive VR without separate app installation
- Learning science integration: design experiments testing specific features of immersive representation (spatial manipulation, real-time parameter adjustment, multi-sensory feedback)
- Collaboration with learning scientists; consider partnering with a research university that has an education research program
- Diverse learner populations: studying efficacy across student demographics at an HBCU

**Budget strategy:** Request up to $900,000 over 3 years. Budget should include a co-PI or consultant in learning sciences/educational psychology, graduate student researchers (if partnering with a doctoral institution), VR hardware for testing, and rigorous experimental design.

**Timeline:** Check NSF RITEL page for next submission window (previously November; a new solicitation may be issued).

---

### 4. Meta Immersive Learning Fund

**Why this is compelling:** Meta is investing $150 million in immersive learning and has specifically partnered with Morehouse College (an HBCU) for VR education. ChemSim's planned WebXR/VR mode would run on Meta Quest headsets, making this a natural industry partnership. Meta provides both funding and hardware.

**What the proposal should emphasize:**
- ChemSim as a showcase application for Meta Quest in HBCU chemistry education
- The browser-based architecture means ChemSim works on Quest's built-in browser, no app store approval needed
- Potential for ChemSim to be a reference implementation for molecular science education on Quest
- Student engagement data and testimonials from pilot deployments
- Willingness to integrate with Meta's education platform and contribute to their content ecosystem

**How to pursue:** This is not a traditional grant application. Contact Meta for Education directly through their partnership inquiry channels. Reference the existing Morehouse College partnership as precedent. Request a hardware grant (Quest headsets for classroom deployment) along with development funding.

**Timeline:** Rolling; initiate contact immediately.

---

### 5. Alfred P. Sloan Foundation (Exploratory Grantmaking in Technology)

**Why this is a good fit:** The Sloan Foundation funds early-stage technology prototyping and proof-of-concept projects, especially tools that benefit the research and education community. Their recent $200K grant to SLAS for laboratory automation education standards demonstrates interest in this space. The Foundation's rolling submission and letter-of-inquiry process is low-barrier.

**What the proposal should emphasize:**
- ChemSim as an emerging open-source tool at the intersection of computational chemistry and education technology
- The potential to establish new standards for interactive molecular simulation in undergraduate chemistry curricula
- Open-source sustainability model and community-building plan
- Alignment with Sloan's interest in leveraging technology to benefit the research community and STEM education

**How to pursue:** Send a two-page letter of inquiry to the Foundation describing ChemSim, its educational goals, the target community (HBCU students and beyond), and the requested budget. Sloan awards approximately 200 grants per year totaling ~$80 million.

**Timeline:** Rolling; submit letter of inquiry as soon as ready.

## Recently Funded Similar Projects

### 1. Alcorn State University / Lobaki -- "ELITE in STEM" (NSF HBCU-UP TIP)

- **Award:** ~$400,000
- **Duration:** 3 years
- **Description:** "Targeted Infusion Project: Elevate Learning with Interactive Technology Enhancements (ELITE) in STEM." Lobaki developed 14 immersive VR chemistry experiences for Alcorn State (an HBCU), enabling students to interact with 3D molecular structures, simulate real-time chemical reactions, and explore complex chemistry concepts.
- **Key detail:** All 14 experiences published under Creative Commons license.
- **Relevance to ChemSim:** This is the closest precedent. ChemSim is pursuing a similar goal (interactive molecular visualization for HBCU chemistry students) but with a browser-based rather than VR-only approach, lowering the hardware barrier.

### 2. Morehouse College -- VR Classroom Initiative (Meta Partnership)

- **Funding:** Meta hardware and platform support
- **Description:** Morehouse launched VR-based courses through Meta's education partnership beta program, making it one of the first HBCUs to offer full courses in virtual reality.
- **Relevance to ChemSim:** Demonstrates Meta's willingness to partner with HBCUs and provides a model for ChemSim's VR deployment path.

### 3. HBCU STEM US Research Center (NSF, via Morehouse College)

- **Award:** $9,000,000
- **Duration:** 5 years
- **Description:** NSF-funded center studying STEM education and career success linked to HBCUs, with participation from up to 50 HBCUs.
- **Relevance to ChemSim:** Potential partner for dissemination and research on ChemSim's impact across multiple HBCUs.

### 4. Cheyney University -- CARE-U (NSF HBCU-UP)

- **Description:** Computational Applications Research Engagement for Undergraduates program, funding 13 Cheyney students in computer vision and audio analysis research, with collaboration with Drexel University.

### 6. SUNY Geneseo -- Chemistry CURE Project (NSF IUSE)

- **Award:** $300,000
- **Duration:** 3 years
- **Description:** Developed course-based undergraduate research experiences (CUREs) across the chemistry curriculum.
- **Relevance to ChemSim:** Model for IUSE proposal structure in chemistry education innovation.

## Draft Letter of Intent Paragraph

The following paragraph can be adapted for letters of intent across multiple mechanisms:

> ChemSim is an open-source, browser-based molecular interaction simulator being developed
> at Cheyney University of Pennsylvania, the nation's oldest HBCU, to transform how
> undergraduate students learn fundamental chemistry concepts such as intermolecular forces,
> chemical bonding, and molecular geometry. By providing interactive, real-time 3D
> visualizations that students can manipulate directly in a web browser on any device,
> ChemSim eliminates the hardware and cost barriers that prevent many students at
> under-resourced institutions from engaging with computational chemistry tools. The
> simulator supports progressive complexity from introductory through advanced coursework
> and includes planned WebXR integration for immersive virtual reality experiences on
> consumer headsets. All code and educational content will be released under open-source
> and Creative Commons licenses, enabling free adoption by other HBCUs and minority-serving
> institutions nationwide. Initial pilot deployment at Cheyney will include rigorous
> assessment of student learning outcomes, engagement, and retention in gateway chemistry
> courses, generating evidence to support broader dissemination and sustained development
> through community contribution.

## Additional Opportunities

### Programs to Monitor

- **NSF S-STEM (Scholarships in STEM):** Next deadline March 3, 2026; awards up to $5M for scholarships and support to low-income STEM students. ChemSim could be embedded in a larger S-STEM proposal.
- **NSF Broadening Participation in Chemistry (CHE-BP):** Supports efforts across the chemistry career pathway; rolling or annual deadlines. Could fund ChemSim as a tool for broadening participation.
- **Dept. of Education Title III (Strengthening HBCUs):** Formula grants to HBCUs; Cheyney likely already receives these. Could fund ChemSim-related activities as part of broader institutional improvement.
- **Google.org AI/STEM Education Grants:** Google.org has funded $75M+ in AI education initiatives and $10M in robotics programs. Contact via google.org; grants are typically by invitation or RFP.
- **VictoryXR Grant Assistance:** VictoryXR (a VR education company) provides grant-writing assistance to institutions seeking funding for VR labs; see [victoryxr.com/grant-assistance](https://www.victoryxr.com/grant-assistance/).

### Strategies to Strengthen Any Proposal

1. **Build a collaboration network.** Partner with a research university (e.g., Drexel, as in the CARE-U precedent) for educational research expertise and co-PI relationships.
2. **Leverage Cheyney's track record.** The CARE-U award demonstrates institutional grant capacity; cite it explicitly.
3. **Emphasize the open-source model.** Reviewers value sustainability and broad impact; open-source licensing shows the project benefits extend beyond one institution.
4. **Collect pilot data now.** Even informal classroom testing with a prototype generates evidence that dramatically strengthens proposals.
5. **Pursue multiple mechanisms simultaneously.** The HBCU-UP TIP and Sloan LOI have different timelines and can be pursued in parallel without conflict.
6. **Account for the current funding climate.** Given federal uncertainty, diversify across federal (NSF, NIH, DOE) and private (HHMI, Sloan, Meta, Google) sources.
